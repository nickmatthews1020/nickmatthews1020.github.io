Estimating the Laffer Curve¶

Final Project Tutorial by Nicholas Matthews¶

Introduction¶

Coined in 1984 by economist Arthur Laffer, the Laffer Curve is a theoretical function that shows the relationship between effective tax rate, and tax revenue collected by the government. While it is often simply thought that a higher tax rate will increase tax revenue, this is not always the case. Logically, it is clear that an extreme tax rate of zero percent will result in zero tax revenue. Similarly, the opposie extreme rate of one hundred percent will also result in zero tax revenue, as the supply of labor in a free market will vanish. These two facts combined with Rolle's Theorem from calculus, imply that somewhere between these two extreme tax rates, of zero and one hundred percent, there is a maximum tax revenue. This universal maximum of tax revenue is the phenomenon captured in the Laffer curve.

While the existence of a maximum tax revenue for a given tax rate is widely recognized, the effective tax rate for which this maximum occurs is not. However, most economics reports and studies estimate it to occur close to the 60-70% range. Determining the real tax rate value for the maximum is an important issue, as it can be used in policy; importantly, if the tax rate of a country is on the right side of this maximum, decreasing tax rate would actually increase tax revenue, which is mutually beneficial for all. More reading on the Laffer Curve can be found at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laffer_curve.

This tutorial through the data science pipeline will attempt to model the Laffer curve and locate this maximum, while demonstrating many of the meaningful tools and mechanisms of the data science process.

Data Management¶

Now that we have both tables of data, we need to combine them into a singular dataframe that we can do analysis on. However, first, we must determine which countries and years occur in both tables. This is easy in this case. The data on tax revenue is much more extensive, covering many more countries than the rate data, and ranging from 1990 to 2019. The data on tax rate is much narrower, as only data is recorded for the OECD countries, and only from 2000 to 2019. Thus, we can simply take the overlapping data, that is, the data from OECD countries between 2000 to 2019. Additionally, we can select only the data columns that pertain to our analysis.

def rates_type(row, lst):
    s = row['ALL_IN']
    if (s == "ALL_IN_RATE_SING_NO_CH"):
        lst[2] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "ALL_IN_RATE_SING_TWO_CH"):
        lst[3] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "ALL_IN_RATE_MAR_NO_CH"):
        lst[4] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "ALL_IN_RATE_MAR_TWO_CH"):
        lst[5] = row['Value']
    return lst

def revenues_type(row, lst):
    s = row['TAX']
    if (s == "TOTALTAX"):
        lst[6] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "1000"):
        lst[7] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "1100"):
        lst[8] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "1110"):
        lst[9] = row['Value']
    elif (s == "1120"):
        lst[10] = row['Value']
    return lst

Exploratory Data Analysis¶

Now that we have properly tidied our combined data and calculated rate averages, we can begin some exploratory data analysis. To start, let's take a look at some simple plots of the rate average against two of the measures of tax revenue, specifically we will focus on the total tax revenue and the tax revenue on individuals, to capture a total effect, and also to isolate the effect of effective income tax rate changes. We will also plot a linear regression on the plotted data to highlight any trends.

As we can see, these two plots clearly show a positive correlation between the tax revenue and tax rate, as can be seen with the positive slope of both linear regressions. Moreover, since the range of tax rate average is less than 40, it should not be the case that a negative correlation appears. Additionally, particularly in the first plot of total tax revenue, it does appear that there is some sort of diminishing marginal effect of tax rate, as many of the data points on the left end of the distribution appear above the regression line, but many of the points on the right end appear below it.

While the trends that we expected to see in the Laffer curve have been verified by this initial data analysis, there is also the issue of any possible correlation between year and tax revenue. While the relationship between tax revenue and rate shown in the Laffer curve is intended to reflect the actual effect of tax rate on other variables, such as labor supply or entrepreneurship, any correlation between the year and tax revenue may convolude the effect that we are interested in. To deal with this potential issue, we must first analyze the distribution of tax revenues by year.

Machine Learning¶

While some exploratory analysis has verified the initial positive correlation implied by the Laffer curve, in addition to confirming that there is no need to standardize our tax revenues by year, to actually generate a model of the Laffer curve, we will need to utilize machine learning. Specifically, we will be using sklearn to implement a nonlinear regression, and test different degrees of a polynomial regression, using a portion of the tax data for training each model and a portion for testing it. Once we have done this, we will also want to analyze the results of the various regressions to see which is a best estimate for the Laffer curve, without overfitting to the data and while minimizing the complexity of the model. For this analysis, we will only be using the total tax revenue data, as running the nonlinear regressions on individual tax revenues has a similar result.

# arrays used to store data about each polynomial degree model for later analysis
model_degree = []
model_lst = []
RMSE_lst = []
test_score_lst = []

# some of the following code was sourced from Towards Data Science (specifically LASSO values and make_pipeline code)
# Alpha (regularization strength) of LASSO regression
lasso_eps = 0.0001
lasso_nalpha=20
lasso_iter=5000
# the range of polynomial degree for which to generate regressions
degree_min = 1
degree_max = 8
# division of training and testing data
test_set_fraction = 0.2
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(data['Rate Average'], data['Total Tax Revenue'],test_size=test_set_fraction)

# adjusts shape of these arrays for use in fitting the models in the next step
X_train = X_train.values.reshape(-1,1)
X_test = X_test.values.reshape(-1,1)

# Make a pipeline model with polynomial transformation and LASSO regression with cross-validation, run it for increasing degree of polynomial (complexity of the model)
for degree in range(degree_min,degree_max+1):
    model = make_pipeline(PolynomialFeatures(degree, interaction_only=False), LassoCV(eps=lasso_eps,n_alphas=lasso_nalpha,max_iter=lasso_iter,
normalize=True,cv=5,  fit_intercept = False))
    model.fit(X_train,y_train)
    test_pred = np.array(model.predict(X_test))
    RMSE=np.sqrt(np.sum(np.square(test_pred-y_test)))
    test_score = model.score(X_test,y_test)
    
    model_degree.append(degree)
    model_lst.append(model)
    RMSE_lst.append(RMSE)
    test_score_lst.append(test_score)

Now that we have non-linear models, with polynomial degree ranging from 1 to 8, we need to determine which model best is the best estimate for the Laffer curve. We can do this by plotting the progression of the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and the R-Squared values resulting from the test data as the degree of the model was increased.

These plots demonstrate a relative stability in both RMSE and R-squared values between models of one and two degrees, but a distinct growth in RMSE for polynomials of the third degree and after. In the case of the R-squared values, after the second degree, the value falls below zero. This is not normally possible, but in this case, where we have forced the regression through the origin (representing a tax rate and revenue of zero), it implies that the models are a poor fit for the model. While there is little difference, and in fact a drop in R-squared and a rise in RMSE, between models of degree one and two, the quadratic model captures the eventual return of tax revenue to zero.

More information about how to perform nonlinear regressions using machine learning can be found at https://towardsdatascience.com/machine-learning-with-python-easy-and-robust-method-to-fit-nonlinear-data-19e8a1ddbd49.

Conclusions¶

Now that we have run our polynomial regressions for tax revenue on tax rate, we can come to some conclusions about our analysis relative to the Laffer curve. To start, while the polynomial regressions did not show significant difference between the simple and the quadratic regressions, the quadratic is much more useful in modeling the curve because it is capable of capturing the curve's return to zero. Moreover, we were able to see that our model of the Laffer curve should not have more than two degrees, as the R-squared rapidly began to fall, and the RMSE rapidly began to rise. Now, we can graph the two degree polynomial regression with the data.

With respect to this model, it appears that the Laffer curve' maximum appears at around a 40 percent tax rate. This does not line up with established estimates, which put the peak at 60-70 percent. However, it is important to keep in mind that our model of the Laffer curve only models the effect of tax rate on tax revenue for countries in the OECD, and not all countries around the globe. Countries in the OECD tend to be economically developed with relatively liberal and democratic governments, and these factors could alter degree to which tax rate affects tax revenue. Moreover, previous economic estimates of the curve may have controlled for other factors and influences, whereas ours relied on the simple relationship between the rate and revenue, after assuring that we need not standardize by year. Additionally, this lower tax rate peak may have occurred because, unlike how we were able to force the regressions through the origin, we are not able to force it through the point representing a tax rate of 100 percent and revenue of zero, as doing so may require a more complex model than we can generate using polynomial regression.

However, while our estimated model of the Laffer curve may not capture the real tax rate turning point for revenue, we have been able to confirm the form of the Laffer curve as being quadratic. Finally, our model does have the same policy conclusion that previous estimates would dictate, and which an almost unanimous body of economists agree: the current tax rate of the U.S., as for any country in the OECD data, is not on the right side of the maximum, meaning that decreasing the tax rate would certainly also decrease the tax revenue collected by the government.

While this tutorial may have fallen short of replicating previous estimates of the Laffer curve, I hope that it leaves you more informed on the data science process and given you insight into its application.
